The Complete Guide to Goods and Services Tax (GST) in India (2026 Edition)

The Goods and Services Tax (GST) is the most transformative indirect tax reform implemented in India since Independence. Rolled out on July 1, 2017, the GST regime fundamentally altered the economic landscape of the country by replacing a complex, cascading web of multiple central and state taxes—including Central Excise Duty, Service Tax, Value Added Tax (VAT), Entry Tax, and Luxury Tax—with a single, unified tax structure. This transition effectively unified India's massive population into a single common market governed by the philosophy of "One Nation, One Tax."

Understanding the 2026 GST Slabs

In order to balance revenue generation with public welfare, the GST Council of India categorised goods and services into a multi-tier tax structure. As of 2026, the active primary tax slabs are 5%, 12%, 18%, and 28%, along with a 0% nil-rated category for essential survival items.

  • 0% (Nil Rate): Applies to absolute daily essentials to protect the poorest demographics. This includes fresh fruits, vegetables, unpackaged milk, natural honey, eggs, and basic educational services.
  • 5% Slab: Applied to basic necessities and mass-consumption goods. This includes items like packaged food, footwear priced under ₹1,000, life-saving medicines, and economy-class air travel.
  • 12% Slab: Covers moderately essential items such as processed foods, butter, cheese, smartphones priced under specific thresholds, and non-AC restaurant food.
  • 18% Slab: The most standard and widely applied rate. It captures the vast majority of services (IT services, financial services, telecom), as well as consumer goods like hair oil, soap, toothpaste, and electronics like monitors and televisions.
  • 28% Slab: Reserved for luxury goods and "demerit" goods. This includes automobiles, premium motorcycles, aerated drinks, air conditioners, large appliances, and tobacco products. Certain items in this slab also attract an additional compensation cess.

CGST, SGST, and IGST Explained

Because India operates as a federal republic, tax revenue must be shared between the Central Government and the individual State Governments. Therefore, the GST framework is split into three distinct components depending on where the transaction takes place.

If a transaction occurs Intra-State (the buyer and seller are located within the exact same state, e.g., a shop in Hyderabad selling to a customer in Warangal), the tax is divided equally into two halves. Half goes to the Central Government as CGST (Central Goods and Services Tax), and the other half goes to the State Government as SGST (State Goods and Services Tax). For example, on an 18% slab item, 9% is charged as CGST and 9% as SGST.

If a transaction occurs Inter-State (the buyer and seller are in different states, e.g., a manufacturer in Telangana selling to a distributor in Maharashtra), a unified tax called IGST (Integrated Goods and Services Tax) is applied. In this case, the full 18% is collected by the Central Government via IGST, which then handles the administrative task of routing the state’s share to the destination state (the state where the goods are ultimately consumed).
